What It Is

A role-based framework: for each sales role it defines the competencies and the underlying skills sellers need, sets the standard for what "good" looks like at each level, and gives them a structured way to build, demonstrate, and be measured on that capability — building on EY's existing curriculum and filling gaps with new content where needed.

The Purpose — Why We're Doing It

  • Replace wide variability with one consistent way of selling across regions and service lines
  • Shift from measuring course completion to measuring demonstrated capability
  • Give sellers a clear, transparent path to develop — and eventually to advance
  • Drive adoption people buy into ("what's in it for me") — ultimately to win more deals

The History — How We Got Here

  • A long-standing goal of Julie Branicki's sales organization; accelerated once leadership aligned at the summit
  • SalesGlobe engaged to design the framework and interview leaders across the firm
  • Anchored on Brilliant Basics and existing programs — extended with new content where gaps exist
  • Piloting with the US Core team (~300 sellers), then scaling globally to all roles and levels
We can't just focus on the learning — we need the application, demonstrated behavior, measurement, and change management, or we won't move the needle the way we intend to.

Current Situation

  • High variability in sales execution across a globally distributed organization
  • ~50% of the workforce are new joiners; prior certification momentum has stalled
  • Inconsistent adoption of sales process, tools, and language across markets
  • No defined path for sellers to build and demonstrate capability

The Approach

  • Role-calibrated model. Defines required skills and behaviors by role, not one-size-fits-all
  • Proficiency standards. Foundation / Practicing / Gold Standard expectations at each level
  • Learning pathways. Maps existing curriculum to development needs, role by role
  • Builds on what exists. Extends EY's Brilliant Basics and sales enablement investments
A curriculum tells people what to attend. A Proficiency Pathway defines what they must demonstrate.
That shift moves EY from measuring participation to measuring demonstrated capability — and connects development directly to career progression.

EY is getting one consistent way to define, measure, and grow sales capability — a role-calibrated Proficiency Pathway that every seller moves through, built on EY's existing L&D investment.

A Six-Month Cadence

Formal structured review with the leader twice a year. Three inputs required — self-assessment, manager alignment, and KPI thresholds. All three must clear; no single input is sufficient.

An Ongoing Rhythm

Development runs through existing meetings — pipeline calls, account reviews, 1:1s. Manager and coach give informal feedback between formal gates. The job is where capability builds.

A Self-Assessment

Skill-by-skill checklist against behaviors defined for the role and level. The individual notes evidence and flags where they need support — the working document for the manager conversation.

Advancement Requirements

Evaluated against the behavioral level for assigned skills at that role. Not every skill is an advancement gate — some are purely developmental.

Where It Connects

Standing feeds into talent reviews and bench-strength conversations. Succession slates for larger accounts and priority pursuits require demonstrated standing — a consistent, evidence-based input beyond manager opinion.

The Readiness Score

A single weighted percentage — agreed-upon ratings measured against required levels, weighted by each skill's importance. 100% means the seller meets the required level on every skill. Gaps on high-weight skills have the greatest impact on the score.

Roll-Out: Embed & Sustain

Change management is not a phase — it runs across all six. Framing the Pathway as career development rather than performance management, activating peer champions, and addressing resistance proactively are what carry adoption through every step.

The Pathway does not start from scratch. EY has invested across Brilliant Basics, the Sales Certification Program, and 6+ active programs.

Current — Curriculum-Based

  • Complete the module, earn the badge
  • Skills assumed from course completion
  • Knowledge tested by quiz scores
  • Development happens off the desk, away from real work
  • Completion rate is the measure of success

Recommended — Capability-Based

  • Skills demonstrated on live accounts and pursuits
  • Evidence reviewed by a coach decides standing
  • Effort measured by applied behavior, not attendance
  • Development embedded in the rhythm of business
  • Behavior change is the measure of success

The First Step: An Audit

Map existing content to the 24 skills, then decide where we build on what exists and where new learning is needed. People forget ~66% of new information within 24 hours without reinforcement — capability sticks through spaced practice on real work.

The manager's job is to build capability, not just measure it — an ongoing development relationship anchored by the assessment, not a once-a-year evaluation.

Coaching Expectations

  • Before: rate each skill ahead of the calibration meeting — preparation is the standard
  • During: lead the conversation; review evidence; agree on levels; co-create the plan
  • After: coach to the plan via field observation, targeted feedback, and check-ins
  • Mid-year: a formal check — where are we, what's working, what needs to shift

Preparing a Manager to Coach

  • Know the tool: how the workbook works and what each output means
  • Lead the conversation: run a calibration; handle rating disagreements
  • Give feedback: specific, behavioral, tied to a skill gap
  • Practice first: role-play scenarios before any real conversation

Coaching the Coach

  • Coaching playbook: what to observe and ask, skill by skill
  • Pulse survey: flags struggling managers before the cycle ends
  • Calibration session: managers align on standards, learn from peers
  • Team heat map: managers see aggregate Readiness and prioritize coaching
  • Named program owner: a single point of contact between touchpoints
