The Case for a Proficiency Pathway
Why EY is moving from measuring participation to measuring demonstrated capability.

Current Situation

  • High variability in sales execution across a globally distributed organization
  • ~50% of the workforce are new joiners; prior certification momentum has stalled
  • Inconsistent adoption of sales process, tools, and language across markets
  • No defined path for sellers to build and demonstrate capability

The Approach

  • Role-calibrated model. Defines required skills and behaviors by role, not one-size-fits-all
  • Proficiency standards. Foundation / Practicing / Gold Standard expectations at each level
  • Learning pathways. Maps existing curriculum to development needs, role by role
  • Builds on what exists. Extends EY's Brilliant Basics and sales enablement investments
A curriculum tells people what to attend. A Proficiency Pathway defines what they must demonstrate.
That shift moves EY from measuring participation to measuring demonstrated capability — and connects development directly to career progression.

Six-Month Cadence

Formal structured review with the leader twice a year. Three inputs required — self-assessment, manager alignment, and KPI thresholds. All three must clear.

Ongoing Rhythm

Development runs through existing meetings — pipeline calls, account reviews, 1:1s. Curriculum supports the journey; the job is where capability builds.

Self-Assessment

Skill-by-skill checklist against behaviors defined for the role and level. Becomes the working document for the manager alignment conversation.

Advancement Requirements

Evaluated against the behavioral level for assigned skills. Not every skill is an advancement gate — some are purely developmental.

The Readiness Score

A single weighted percentage — agreed ratings measured against required levels, weighted by each skill's importance. 100% means the seller meets the required level on every skill. Gaps on high-weight skills have the greatest impact on the score.

The Pathway does not start from scratch. EY has invested across Brilliant Basics, the Sales Certification Program, and 6+ active programs.

Current — Curriculum-Based

  • Complete the module, earn the badge
  • Skills assumed from course completion
  • Knowledge tested by quiz scores
  • Development happens off the desk, away from real work
  • Completion rate is the measure of success

Recommended — Capability-Based

  • Skills demonstrated on live accounts and pursuits
  • Evidence reviewed by a coach decides standing
  • Effort measured by applied behavior, not attendance
  • Development embedded in the rhythm of business
  • Behavior change is the measure of success

The First Step: An Audit

Map existing content to the 24 skills, then decide where we build on what exists and where new learning is needed. People forget ~66% of new information within 24 hours without reinforcement — capability sticks through spaced practice on real work.

The manager's job is to build capability, not just measure it — an ongoing relationship anchored by the assessment, not a once-a-year event.

Coaching Expectations

  • Before: rate each skill ahead of the calibration meeting
  • During: lead the conversation; agree on levels; co-create the plan
  • After: coach to the plan via field observation and feedback
  • Mid-year: a formal check on progress and what needs to shift

Preparing a Manager to Coach

  • Know the tool: how the workbook works and what each output means
  • Lead the conversation: run a calibration, handle rating disagreements
  • Give feedback: specific, behavioral, tied to a skill gap
  • Practice first: role-play scenarios before any real conversation

Coaching the Coach

  • Coaching playbook: what to observe and ask, skill by skill
  • Pulse survey: flags struggling managers before the cycle ends
  • Calibration session: managers align on standards, learn from peers
  • Named program owner: a single point of contact between touchpoints
